Bio


I was born in Yancheng, Jiangsu province of China, in 1991.

With an interest in artificial intelligence, I came to Beihang University1 to study computer science as an undergraduate. After several years of study, I felt both exited, as CS is such an profound subject, and disappointed, as I realized that most research conducted in my department were more data science than intelligence.

One day passing through a deep hallway, a piece of thought hit upon me: the retinas are two-dimensional membranes, how could I get the vivid feeling of depth simply from the 2D images on the retinas? With little knowledge in neuroscience and psychology, this question arrested me. For the first time I realized that human vision is not a naive task I have been taking granted for. Instead of the images on the retinas, it must be some other things constructed from these images that we see.

This realization fascinated me. A succession of questions bumped into my head: What are these other things? Where do they live, and in what format? Do different people construct them differently? If so, doesn’t that mean we see differently even given the same thing? These questions opened the door to a field I never arrived: the world of mind. A friend told me that this is the subject psychologists research. So I read some books about psychology, and finally decided to follow these questions in graduate school.

Fortunately, I got the chance to work with Jia Liu, an expert in object recognition, in Beijing Normal University. Until then did I get to know that the other things are termed as representation, the process of construction is called computation, and the way brain physically works is called implementation. I also learned that the questions haunted me are among the fundamental problems.

Now having some experience of research, I know these are hard and elusive problems, which may often frustrate those who try to solve them. But I am happy enough in the process approaching them, when I keep asking, trying, even failing, and again expecting. These problems still haunt me like they did when I first met them. And I cannot help envisioning the day we solve them to the extent that, combined with engineering, we can create new ways of expressing, recording, and reenacting our mind.


  1. Beihang University, formerly known as Beijing University of Aeronautics and Astronautics, is among the top engineering universities in China, whose computer science is consistently ranked within top 5 by the Ministry of Education (4th in 2012 and 3rd in 2007)↩
