Research

Probabilistic Atlas of Six Face-selective Regions

Face-selective regions (FSRs) are among the most widely studied functional regions. However, individual variability of the FSRs has not been well quantified. Here we use functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) to localize the FSRs and quantify their spatial and functional variability on 202 healthy adults. The occipital face area (OFA), posterior and anterior fusiform face area (pFFA and aFFA), posterior continuation of superior temporal sulcus (pcSTS), posterior and anterior STS (pSTS and aSTS) were delineated manually for each individual. A probabilistic atlas was constructed to characterize their interindividual variability and revealed that the FSRs were highly variable in location and extent across subjects. The variability of FSRs was further quantified on both functional (i.e., faces selectivity) and spatial (i.e., volume, location of peak activation, and anatomical location) features. Considerable interindividual variability and rightward asymmetry were found in all FSRs on these features. Taking together, our work presents the first effort to comprehensively characterize the variability of FSRs on a large sample of healthy subjects, and invites future work on the origin of the variability, and its relation to individual differences in behavioral performance. Moreover, the probabilistic functional atlas will provide an adequate spatial reference to map the face network.

In order to reliably and efficiently delineate functional regions for this large sample of subjects, we designed a delineation pipeline which incorporates the subject-specific response, the group response, and the anatomical reference. To help implementing this pipeline, we developed a software called FreeROI . Initially designed for our atlas labeling, it later developped into an independent and general purpose software for neuroimaging data visualization and analyzing. I am responsible for data structures, interfaces to data access and manipulation, image processing, and some GUI programming of the software.

Find more about our atlas and tools at: Brain Activity Atlas.


Differential Roles of Category Selectivity and Multivariate Pattern in Facial Expression and Identity Recognition

The face-selective region in the posterior superior temporal cortex (pSTS) has recently been found to be involved in both dynamic aspects, such as facial expression, and static aspects, such as facial identity, of faces. However, whether facial identity and facial expression are represented in the same way in this region remains unclear. One limitation of previous research is that when comparing responses to expression and identity in pSTS, only univariate activation but no mulitvariate patterns were tested, making whether these two measures contain same or different behavior-related information unclear. In this study, we investigated and compared the behavioral significance of univariate measure (category selectivity) and multivariate measure (pattern dissimilarity) in the pSTS as neural predictors of facial identity and expression recognition. Using a passive-view functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) experiment and two behavioral tests, we show a double dissociation between pattern dissimilarity and category selectivity: pattern dissimilarity predicts facial identity recognition, but not expression recognition, while category selectivity predicts expression recognition, but not identity recognition. Such dissociation reveals the behavioral distinctness of multivariate pattern and activation magnitude in face processing of pSTS, and suggests that facial identity and expression are represented differently in the pSTS.


Interplay of Category Selectivity, Within-category Representation of Similarity, and Behavior

Category selectivity may be the most famous and commonly mentioned measure of neural activity in object recognition. However, the computation under and the purpose upon this measure are unclear. To this end, this project tries to get some hints about following questions:

  1. what neural computation is related to category selectivity?
  2. what is computed through category selectivity?

Preliminary results show a interesting link between representation of similarity and category selectivity: in face-selective areas, face selectivity is correlated with representation of similarity among faces, but not scenes, while in scene-selective areas, scene selectivity is correlated with representation of similarity among scenes, but not faces.
